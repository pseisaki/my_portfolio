Wealth Advisor Plan Builder

Role
Product Designer, sole designer on this project
Team
1 PM, 1 lead engineer, 1 data analyst, 2 frontend, 3 backend (AI squad)
Timeframe
3 sprints, shipped June 2026, in active iteration
This product is live and iterating. Numbers below reflect Q2 2026 results, Q3 2026 changes are in progress.

Overview

Plum's chat assistant already helps people save and invest. AI Wealth Plans turns that into a real plan: a goal, a monthly deposit, where the money goes, and a projection of where it leads, built in minutes instead of a form or a paid advisor.

The problem

85% of eligible users never opened the advisor

Two gaps stacked on top of each other: most users never found the advisor, and of those who did, 9 in 10 never saw a finished plan.

  • Retirement questions asked for sensitive info too early, before trust was earned
  • Investment questions deflected to "emergency fund" instead of answering what was asked
  • General saving never asked "saving for what," so plans felt generic
  • Plans showed up as unreadable chat text, not something you could find again

Plan creation is where advice turns into action. A user who fails to create a plan leaves with nothing to show for it, which means fewer activations, fewer deposits, and weaker habits down the line.

Strategy

Primary goal

Move plan save rate from a ~12% baseline toward a 30% target. Fix visibility so plans stop disappearing into chat text.

Secondary goal

Turn plan creation into a way in for people who feel shut out of financial advice. Our own research shows people often rule themselves out of advice altogether, assuming it's only for those with more money. A free, trustworthy plan inside an app people already use is one of the most direct ways to reach them.

Insights

01

Users abandon before a plan is even generated

In session reviews, people dropped off before a plan even appeared. Too many questions up front, confusing or repeated ones, information overload, wrong recommendations, or the assistant simply misreading what someone wanted, any one of these was enough to end the flow with nothing to show for it.

Source: internal plan creation evaluations, qualitative review
02

People will use an AI plan, but only if they can still touch it

We tested two early designs with 10 people: one built the plan automatically, the other let people edit every field. The automatic version was faster to start, but felt generic and out of their hands. The editable version won almost everyone over, not because it looked less like AI, but because people could control their deposit, target date, and allocation. Editable deposits and target date were non-negotiable, full stop.

Source: Wealth Plans concept test, December 2025, n=10
03

Advice is undersold to the people who need it most

A wider survey and interviews on financial advice kept surfacing the same want: reassurance, not bigger returns. People want to stop worrying about money, not optimize it. Many rule themselves out of advice altogether, assuming it's only for people with more money. Even people who'd used and left an advisor said the same thing: they'd pay for a one-off check-in, not a subscription.

Source: Financial Advisor Study, May 2026, n=345 survey plus 8 interviews

Decisions

Six changes, built directly from the insights above: get people to the advisor, respect their time once they're in, give them a plan they can compare and edit, and make it obvious what to do next.

Decision 1

Add "Start a Plan" to the homepage's suggested next actions

Why: 85% of people never opened the advisor. Part of that is simple, it had no home besides a buried spot in the bottom nav. Now it's a suggested next action right on the homepage.
Homepage, "Start a Plan" inside suggested next actions
Decision 2

Open the advisor into 4 goal cards, instead of a blank chat

Why: session reviews showed people abandoning at open-ended, unclear questions right at the start. Our concept test also found generic goal templates feel impersonal. So instead of a blank chat or a generic list, the advisor opens with the 4 goals people ask for most.
Advisor opening screen: 4 goal cards
Decision 3

Replace the open conversation with a 3-question modal

Why: people were dropping off over too many, repeated, or unclear questions, and our concept test showed how much trust depends on feeling in control from the start. Now it's capped at 3 questions, no sensitive data, multiple choice with the option to type your own, ranges instead of blank fields.
3-question modal flow, multiple choice plus type your own
Decision 4

Generate 3 plan options instead of 1, then open an editable detail screen

Why: our concept test showed a single automatic plan felt generic and out of people's control, while an editable version won almost everyone over. Now it generates a slow, balanced, and fast-track option, and tapping one opens a full plan you can edit directly.
Slow / balanced / fast-track suggestions, then editable plan detail
Decision 5

Turn "Save plan" into "Continue with setup," with a saved-plan artefact, progress tracking, and info icons

Why: our usability test showed people wanted reassurance and control right at the point of committing. So "Save plan" became "Continue with setup," saved plans get their own home with the next action front and centre, progress tracking so it doesn't disappear, and info icons explaining the deposit split and the projection.
Saved plan artefact: next action, progress tracking, info icons
Decision 6

Run a CRM campaign to people who'd never opened the advisor

Why: no in-product fix solves a gap that starts before someone sees the feature. An email and push campaign to that exact group drove a 140% lift in planning sessions versus a control group, and 85% of them were opening the advisor for the first time.
CRM campaign creative (email / push)

Impact

Sessions nearly doubled week-on-week post-launch: 3,343 to 11,942.
Plan save rate climbed from a 12% baseline to a 15.2% peak.
Plan Viewed to Saved conversion improved from about 48% to 54.4%.
CRM campaign drove +140% more planning sessions vs. control, with 85% first-time planners.
Honest miss: overall adoption is still small, only a few thousand users have a wealth plan today. There's also a measurement problem: funded plans are higher than saved plans. Some people skip saving the plan but still complete the tasks it recommends within 7 days. Plan save rate, the number we've been chasing, may be undercounting how well this actually works.

Takeaways

⚙

Bring engineers in earlier

There was a real gap between the vision and what we could build, and we only found out how big partway through. Next time, engineers see the concept before it's final, not after.

◎

Question the format, not just the flow

I designed around the assumption that people want a goal-first plan. I'd test whether broader financial advice and coaching is what they actually want before building the rest around it.

⧉

Sequence releases, don't bundle them

We wanted to ship too many things at once, which made it impossible to track the impact of any single decision. Next time I'd push the team to prioritise and stagger releases from the start.
